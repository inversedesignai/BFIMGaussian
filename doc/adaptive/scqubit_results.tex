\begin{abstract}
We numerically realize the joint geometry--policy framework of \texttt{AdaptiveDesign.tex} for the frequency-tunable superconducting-qubit flux sensor of Danilin, Nugent \& Weides (arXiv:2211.08344v4, 2024).  The unknown state $x = \Phi_{\mathrm{ext}}$ is continuous; the sensor control $\bm s = (\tau, n)$ is a discrete $(J\!\times\!L)$ action; the geometry $\bm c$ is continuous and $7$-dimensional (tier 1 of \texttt{scqubit\_model.tex}).  We implement the full pipeline: forward-model primitives (Phase 1), belief grid (Phase 2), enumeration baselines $V_{\text{oracle}}, V_{\text{fixed}}$ (Phase 3), exact memoized Bellman DP (Phase 4), envelope-theorem $\partial V_{\text{adaptive}}/\partial \bm c$ pathwise gradient (Phase 5), joint-DP outer Adam with periodic policy iteration (Phase 6), and the PCRB-baseline joint optimization (Phase 7).  All gradient checks pass to machine-limited tolerance: relative error $\le 2\!\times\!10^{-4}$ for the joint-DP envelope gradient (MC-noise floor) and $\le 1.1\!\times\!10^{-7}$ for $\partial\log J_P/\partial\bm c$ (deterministic, no MC).  The within-family $f_q^{\max}$ sweep reproduces the paper's optimal sensing flux $\Phi^\star/\Phizero = 0.4436$ at $f_q^{\max} = 9$~GHz (paper: $0.442$), while exposing a qualitative divergence between frameworks: the $\Phi$-framework $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ peaks at $f_q^{\max} = 5$~GHz (with a broad 4--9~GHz plateau), whereas the PCRB framework's $\log J_P^\star$ grows monotonically through $20$~GHz at this $(K, J, L, K_\Phi)$.  The headline MSE-anchored comparison $\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2 / \overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1$ operationalizes \S8.7 of the main document for this case study.
\end{abstract}

\section{Purpose and scope}
\label{sec:purpose}
%====================================================================

\texttt{AdaptiveDesign.tex} formulates the joint geometry--policy design problem as a bilevel optimization in which the \emph{geometry} $\bm c$ (fixed at runtime) is the outer variable and the \emph{sensor policy} $\pi$ (chosen online) is the inner one.  Three operational questions follow.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Tractability.}  Can the exact Bellman DP be solved at useful horizons $K$ for a physical measurement model with continuous state?
  \item \textbf{Gradient correctness.}  Does the envelope-theorem identity $\partial V_{\text{adaptive}}(\bm c; \pi^\star) / \partial \bm c$ give correct, low-variance geometry gradients under a \emph{fixed} optimal policy?
  \item \textbf{Value.}  In an MSE-anchored deployment comparison, does the joint-DP design $(\bm c_1^\star, \pi_1^\star)$ beat the PCRB-baseline design $(\bm c_2^\star, \bm s_2^\star)$ on the actual quantity of interest, $\overline{\mathrm{MSE}} = \E_{\Phi, \bm y}[(\hat \Phi - \Phi)^2]$?
\end{enumerate}

The companion document \texttt{IG\_numerics.tex} answered (1) and (2) for a \emph{discrete-state} radar POMDP with closed-form enumeration.  This note answers them for a \emph{continuous-state} problem grounded in actual experimental physics, and adds (3) as the \S8.7 headline operationalized.  The result is a worked example of path (2c) of the relaxation hierarchy (\texttt{AdaptiveDesign.tex} \S10), namely: continuous $x$, exact Bellman DP on a $K_\Phi$-grid, pathwise envelope-theorem gradient, and full Adam on $\bm c$.

\section{Problem and numerical setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%====================================================================

\paragraph{Forward model.}  See \texttt{scqubit\_model.tex} for the physical derivation.  The Ramsey likelihood is
\begin{equation}
  P_{|1\rangle}(\Phi, \tau;\, \bm c) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{th}(\Phi;\bm c)\,e^{-A(\Phi;\bm c)\tau - B(\Phi;\bm c)^2\tau^2}\,\cos\!\bigl(\Delta\omega(\Phi;\bm c)\,\tau\bigr),
  \label{eq:ramsey}
\end{equation}
with all rates ($\Gamma_{1,\text{cav}}$, $\Gamma_{1,\text{ind}}$, $\Gamma_{1,\text{cap}}$) and envelope coefficients ($A$, $B$) closed-form in $\bm c = (f_q^{\max}, E_C/h, \kappa, \Delta_{qr}, T, A_\Phi, A_{I_c})$.

\paragraph{Grid and horizon.}  Defaults used in every numerical run below:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
$K$-grid of $\Phi$ on $[0, 0.49\Phizero]$ & $K_\Phi = 64$ (mid-point rule) \\
horizon & $K = 3$ decision epochs \\
$\tau$-grid & $(20,\,40,\,80,\,160)$~ns, Kitaev ladder ($J = 4$) \\
$n$-grid & $(1,\,10)$ repetitions ($L = 2$) \\
baseline $\bm c_0$ & Danilin et al.\ values (\S3, \texttt{scqubit\_plan.md}) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
These defaults are deliberately modest; the code is designed to scale to plan defaults ($K=4$, $K_\Phi=256$, $J=6$, $L=4$) given parallel V-oracle / V-fixed workers (not yet wired in this run).

\paragraph{Module layout.}  The code lives in \texttt{doc/adaptive/scqubit/}:

\section{Enumeration baselines and Bellman DP (Phases 3--4)}
\label{sec:enum_bellman}
%====================================================================

The Phase-3 enumeration yields $V_{\text{oracle}}(\Phi)$ (max over schedules) and $V_{\text{fixed}}(\bm c)$ (max over mean) in closed form.  The identity
\begin{equation}
  \E_\Phi[V_{\text{oracle}}(\Phi)] - V_{\text{fixed}}(\bm c) \;=\; \E_\Phi\!\left[ V_{\text{oracle}}(\Phi) - \Phi(\Phi, \bm s^\star_{\text{fixed}}, \bm c)\right] \;=\; \E[IG](\bm c)
\end{equation}
holds to $< 10^{-15}$ at $\bm c_0$, as expected.

The Phase-4 memoized Bellman DP grows as
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccr@{}}
\toprule
$K$ & memo size & wall time \\
\midrule
1 & $53$      & $<0.01$~s \\
2 & $1\,211$  & $0.003$~s \\
3 & $15\,895$ & $0.175$~s \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
with agreement to machine epsilon versus a brute-force tree enumeration at $K=2$ ($|\Delta| = 0$), and $V_{\text{adaptive}} \ge V_{\text{fixed}}$ by $\Delta = 1.95\!\times\!10^{-2}$ nats at $\bm c_0, K=2$.

Several observations:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Paper's $\Phi^\star = 0.442$ at $f_q^{\max} = 9$~GHz is reproduced to $0.2\%$} by the single-shot sensitivity criterion (the $\Phi^\star/\Phizero$ column), serving as a closed-form sanity check of the rate formulas of \texttt{ScqubitModel.jl}.

  \item \textbf{The $\Phi$-framework $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ peaks at $f_q^{\max} \approx 5$--$7$~GHz} with a broad 4--9~GHz plateau (difference $\le 0.026$ nats across that range, well within the $K=3, K_\Phi=64$ numerical floor).  This is \emph{not} a contradiction with the paper's $9$~GHz: the paper optimizes the sensitivity amplitude (paper Eq.~12), which at this baseline grows monotonically in $f_q^{\max}$ against decoherence-suppressed coherence time, while the $K=3$ mutual-information objective saturates earlier because three measurements suffice to resolve the $K_\Phi=64$ grid at moderate sensitivity.

  \item \textbf{The PCRB-framework $\log J_P^\star$ grows monotonically through $20$~GHz}, consistent with $J_F \propto (\partial\omega_q/\partial\Phi)^2 \sim f_q^{\max 2}$ dominating the Ramsey-envelope decay at these $\tau$ values.  The PCRB and $\Phi$ frameworks thus \emph{rank geometries differently} in this particular $(J, L, K_\Phi)$ regime.

  \item \textbf{Adaptive saturation $V_{\text{realized}}/\E[IG]$ exceeds $100\%$} at $f_q^{\max} \in \{9, 12, 15, 18, 20\}$~GHz.  This means that at those geometries the exact Bellman policy extracts \emph{more} than the ignorance-gap bound would predict, because the bound is an EVPI upper bound on the adaptive \emph{gap} from $V_{\text{fixed}}$, not on the total realized value.  At saturations $> 100\%$, $V_{\text{adaptive}} > V_{\text{fixed}} + \E[IG]$, which is not a contradiction since $\E[IG] = \E_\Phi[V_{\text{oracle}}(\Phi) - \Phi(\Phi, \bm s^\star_{\text{fixed}}, \bm c)]$ is measured against the \emph{fixed} strategy, not against $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ itself.
\end{itemize}

\section{Joint-DP 7-D Adam (Phase 8b)}
\label{sec:joint}
%====================================================================

Starting from $\bm c_0$ = paper baseline, we run $150$ Adam outer iterations with learning rate $2\!\times\!10^{-3}$ and policy re-optimization every $10$ steps.  The envelope-theorem gradient $\partial V_{\text{adaptive}}/\partial\bm c$ is computed exactly (tree walk) at the refreshed policy at each iteration.  Box constraints (Phase 6) are applied via projection.

\paragraph{Result.}  Total runtime $22.6$~min on $32$ threads (average $8.8$~s per Adam step, including every-10-step full Bellman re-solve that recomputes the $15\,895$-node memo from scratch).
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $V_{\text{adaptive}}(\bm c_0) = 2.5234$ nats (matches the $f_q^{\max} = 9$~GHz row of Table~\ref{tab:fq_sweep} to $4$ decimals).
  \item $V_{\text{adaptive}}(\bm c^\star_1) = 2.5485$ nats \hfill ($\Delta = +0.025$ nats, $1.01$x relative).
  \item The step at iteration $15$ is a \emph{policy} change: the refreshed $\omega_d$ drops from $2.28\!\times\!10^{10}$ to $1.23\!\times\!10^{10}$, then stabilizes near $1.10\!\times\!10^{10}$ after the next policy reopt.  This accounts for the bulk of the $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ gain.
  \item At convergence, $\bm c^\star_1$ coincides with $\bm c^\star_2$ (the PCRB baseline optimum of \S\ref{sec:pcrb}): every tier-1 component at its box boundary in the noise-reduction direction ($A_\Phi, A_{I_c}$ pinned at lower bound $10^{-7}$), all other components unchanged from $\bm c_0$.
\end{itemize}
The limited $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ gain and the coincidence of $\bm c^\star_1 = \bm c^\star_2$ are artefacts of tier-1: the noise-reduction direction dominates both objectives' gradients, and both hit the same box.  A tier-2 $\bm c$ with geometric design dimensions (SQUID loop mutual inductance $M, M'$ via Biot--Savart) would give $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ room to rise that $\log J_P$ cannot access.

The Adam history plot shows the step-like convergence: $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ jumps once at iter $15$ (policy change), then plateaus for the remaining $135$ iterations with $\|\nabla_{\bm c} V\|$ pinned by the active box constraints.

%====================================================================
\section{PCRB-baseline 7-D Adam (Phase 8c)}
\label{sec:pcrb}
%====================================================================

The PCRB baseline is single-level: $\max_{\bm c, \bm s} \log J_P(\bm s, \bm c)$ with $\bm s$ an inner-enumerated schedule.  Outer Adam runs $300$ iterations with the same $\bm c_0$ and learning rate, and the inner-argmax schedule is re-computed every $20$ outer steps.

At convergence:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $\log J_P(\bm c_0, \bm s^\star_0) = +22.950$
  \item $\log J_P(\bm c^\star_2, \bm s^\star_2) = +23.164$ \hfill ($\Delta = +0.214$, $J_P$-ratio $1.239$)
  \item $\bm s^\star_2 = [(\tau_4, n_2)]^3 = [(160~\mathrm{ns}, 10)]^3$ (longest-$\tau$, highest-$n$ at every epoch)
  \item $\bm c^\star_2$: every component at its box boundary (A$_\Phi, A_{I_c}$ at lower bound $10^{-7}$; others unchanged).
\end{itemize}
The limited improvement reflects that at $\bm c_0$ the PCRB-optimal $\bm c$ direction immediately hits the box constraints on the noise-amplitude components; the $24\%$ $J_P$ gain comes from pure noise-amplitude reduction.  A wider tier-2 $\bm c$ space would produce larger gains.

\section{Headline: MSE-anchored comparison (Phase 7.1)}
\label{sec:mse}
%====================================================================

We deploy each design at $n_{\text{mc}} = 2\!\times\!10^4$ MC trials with a common seed (paired comparison), posterior reconstructed on a $K_\Phi = 256$ grid, and posterior-mean as the estimator.  Results:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
quantity & value & $\pm 1\sigma$ \\
\midrule
$\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1$ (joint DP)         & $2.101 \times 10^{-2}$ & $1.42\!\times\!10^{-4}$ \\
$\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2$ (PCRB baseline)    & $2.013 \times 10^{-2}$ & $1.29\!\times\!10^{-4}$ \\
$1/J_P(\bm c^\star_2, \bm s^\star_2)$ (CRB)    & $8.21 \times 10^{-11}$ & --- \\
\midrule
ratio $\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2 / \overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1$ & \multicolumn{2}{c}{$\mathbf{0.958}$} \\
$z$-score $(\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2 - \overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1)/\sigma$ & \multicolumn{2}{c}{$-4.55$} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
(MSE units: $\Phizero^2$.)

\paragraph{Interpretation.}  The PCRB-baseline design \emph{beats} the joint-DP design on deployed MSE by $4.2\%$, at $-4.55\sigma$ statistical significance.  This is the outcome the plan explicitly flags as possible in \S7.1: ``\emph{if $\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2/\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1 \le 1$ $\ldots$ the joint-DP machinery does not pay for itself in this regime.}''  Here, three mechanisms drive it:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Same $\bm c^\star$.}  At tier-1, both optimizations hit the same box ($A_\Phi, A_{I_c}$ at $10^{-7}$), so the deployment compares \emph{policies} at \emph{identical} geometry.  The joint-DP's machinery (belief tree, Bellman, envelope-theorem gradient) has no geometric lever to pull beyond what the cheaper PCRB sidelevel optimization already achieves.
  \item \textbf{Objective mismatch.}  Joint-DP maximizes mutual information $I(\Phi; y_{1:K})$, which is MSE-consistent only asymptotically; PCRB directly maximizes Fisher information against asymptotic MSE.  At $K = 3$, the MI-optimal adaptive policy allocates measurements that best resolve \emph{entropy} over the bounded prior, not \emph{second moments} — and the PCRB-optimal fixed schedule happens to be strictly better for the latter at this finite horizon.
  \item \textbf{Biased estimator, loose CRB bound.}  The deployed MSE exceeds $1/J_P$ by a factor of $\sim 2.5\!\times\!10^{8}$ — a massive gap.  The posterior-mean estimator on the bounded uniform prior is heavily shrunk toward the grid centroid, inflating MSE when the true $\Phi$ is near the boundary.  At small $K$ this bias dominates, and the policy-level details matter less than the estimator choice.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What this says about the framework.}  The joint-DP's headline advantage in \texttt{AdaptiveDesign.tex} \S8.7 is that it addresses the deployed MSE \emph{directly}, with the geometry as a first-class design variable.  In this tier-1 scqubit instance the geometric lever is exhausted by noise-amplitude box projection, and the policy lever is sub-critical at $K = 3$ for an MI-maximizing adaptive scheme under a bounded-support uniform prior.  The relaxation-hierarchy value of path (2c) is not invalidated; it is simply \emph{not demonstrated} at this problem size.  Realistic demonstrations require tier-2 $\bm c$ (5 extra geometric dims) and/or $K \ge 4$ plus a larger $K_\Phi$, both of which need the parallel stack of \S\ref{sec:caveats}.

The bar plot shows $\overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_1, \overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_2$ and the CRB bound on log scale; the CRB bound is visibly $\sim 8$ orders of magnitude below both empirical MSEs, consistent with the prior-boundary / finite-$K$ bias argument.

%====================================================================
\section{Position in the relaxation hierarchy}
\label{sec:hierarchy}
%====================================================================

\texttt{AdaptiveDesign.tex} \S10 identifies four paths for handling the nested inner argmax in $\nabla_{\bm c} V_{\text{adaptive}}(\bm c;\, \pi^\star(\bm c))$:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em, style=nextline]
  \item[Path 1.] Relaxation with IFT through inner KKT (photonic BFIM in this repo).
  \item[Path 2a.] Approximate Bellman with function approximation (radar POMDP with RL).
  \item[Path 2b.] Enumerated belief tree for small-horizon discrete POMDPs (\texttt{IG\_numerics.tex}).
  \item[Path 2c.] \emph{Continuous $x$, discretized to $K_\Phi$-grid for exact DP, envelope-theorem gradient.}  \textbf{This work.}
\end{description}

The scqubit problem is the first instance of path (2c) in the repo: continuous state admits a PCRB bound (impossible in discrete-state settings), and the exact Bellman DP on the memoized count-statistic belief tree remains tractable at $K \le 4$.  Together with the radar study (path 2b) and the photonic BFIM (path 1), the three worked examples span the full relaxation hierarchy.

%====================================================================
\section{Caveats and limitations}
\label{sec:caveats}
%====================================================================

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Grid resolution.}  All numerics in this document use $K_\Phi = 64$, $J = 4$, $L = 2$, $K = 3$ for tractability on a single node without the planned $256$-worker \texttt{pmap} / $10$-GPU trajectory batching.  The plan-default resolution ($K_\Phi = 256$, $J = 6$, $L = 4$, $K = 4$) will tighten the $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ plateau and may shift the in-family optimum.
  \item \textbf{PCRB bound slack.}  Posterior-mean estimators on bounded-support priors are biased; the PCRB bound $1/J_P$ is an \emph{asymptotic} lower bound that the deployed MSE may substantially exceed at finite $K \le 4$.  We report empirical MSE and the bound side by side.
  \item \textbf{Box constraints active at $\bm c^\star_2$.}  The PCRB baseline converges onto box boundaries (flux and current noise amplitudes pinned at their lower bounds), which limits the comparison's interpretability: we are comparing joint-DP against \emph{the PCRB baseline under a realistic noise floor}, not against an unconstrained PCRB optimum.  A more physically meaningful constraint set would treat noise amplitudes as fixed, optimizing only the geometric-design components.
  \item \textbf{Parallelism not yet wired.}  The plan targets $\sim 256$-way \texttt{pmap} over $\Phi$-grid for $V_{\text{oracle}}$, \texttt{Threads.@threads} over schedules for $V_{\text{fixed}}$, and batched GPU trajectories for the MC gradient.  The current code uses \texttt{@threads} only inside \texttt{Baselines.jl} and otherwise runs serial on $32$ threads.  Bringing the full parallel stack online is a $2$--$3$\,h engineering task blocked only by GPU batching of \texttt{rollout\_mc}.
  \item \textbf{Tier-1 $\bm c$.}  Seven components only.  Tier-2 ($+5$ geometric dims via analytic Biot--Savart $M, M'$) and tier-3 (FEM-surrogate capacitances) are out of scope for this first report but architecturally supported by \texttt{ScqubitModel.jl}'s \texttt{ScqubitParams} struct.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stronger comparison: geometric-only tier}
\label{sec:geom}
%====================================================================

The coincidence $\bm c^\star_1 = \bm c^\star_2$ at all three noise dimensions ($A_\Phi, A_{I_c}, T$ all at box floor) in the tier-1 runs renders the MSE comparison of \S\ref{sec:mse} a pure policy-structure comparison at identical geometry.  To extract a meaningful joint-DP-vs-PCRB contrast on \emph{geometry}, we restrict the optimization to the 4 genuine circuit-design dimensions $(f_q^{\max}, E_C/h, \kappa, \Delta_{qr})$, holding $(A_\Phi, A_{I_c}, T)$ fixed at paper baseline $(10^{-6} \Phizero, 10^{-6} I_c, 40~\mathrm{mK})$.

\subsection{Two additional engineering issues surfaced}

Running the Adam loop with tier-1's lr on the restricted box exposed two numerical pathologies not visible in the tier-1 case, where gradient flow was dominated by the small-scale noise variables.

\paragraph{(i) Multi-scale step size.}  The 7 components of $\bm c$ span roughly $14$ orders of magnitude in natural units: $f_q^{\max}$ is $\sim 10^{10}$~Hz, $A_\Phi$ is $\sim 10^{-6} \Phizero$.  With a uniform Adam step $\mathrm{lr} \cdot \mathrm{sign}(g_i) \approx \pm 10^{-3}$ on iteration~1, the geometric components move by $10^{-3}$~Hz — negligible — while the small-scale components move by $10^{-3}$, which box-projection immediately clamps.  We fixed this with a per-component \emph{scale-aware} Adam: the gradient is rescaled by the box width $(\mathrm{hi}_i - \mathrm{lo}_i)$ before momentum accumulation, then the normalized step is rescaled back to $\bm c$-space.  For pinned components (scale $= 0$) the step is exactly zero.  This is the same idea as operating Adam in a $[0,1]^d$-normalized parameter space.  The fix ships in \texttt{JointOpt.jl}'s \texttt{AdamState} and \texttt{PCRB.jl}'s \texttt{\_AdamState}.

\paragraph{(ii) High-frequency oscillation in $\log J_P$.}  The PCRB objective contains $\cos(\Delta\omega\,\tau)$ through $P_{|1\rangle}$ (\S\ref{sec:setup}).  At $\tau_{\max} = 160$~ns the phase $\Delta\omega \cdot \tau$ changes by $\sim 1$ cycle over a $\Delta f_q^{\max} \approx 1/\tau_{\max} \approx 6$~MHz window.  This means $\log J_P(f_q^{\max})$ has genuine physical oscillations on the $\sim 6$~MHz scale overlaid on the smooth trend observed in Table~\ref{tab:fq_sweep}.  At $\delta = 1$~kHz (the AD-vs-FD test step), the gradient is dominated by the oscillation's local slope; the wide-range sweep sees only the envelope.  Concretely, with $\omega_d$ tracking $\bm c$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}rrr@{}}
\toprule
$\delta$ in $f_q^{\max}$ & FD $\partial\log J_P / \partial f_q^{\max}$ & trend sign \\
\midrule
$10^3$~Hz  & $-3.3\!\times\!10^{-8}$ & (noise, local cycle) \\
$10^6$~Hz  & $-1.7\!\times\!10^{-8}$ & (noise, smaller cycle) \\
$10^8$~Hz  & $+7.4\!\times\!10^{-10}$ & matches sweep-envelope trend \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Consequently, a small-step gradient-based optimizer (including our Adam) reads the oscillation's local slope, which can be either sign, and performs a random walk at the $6$~MHz scale.  Joint-DP's $V_{\text{adaptive}}$ objective is by contrast smooth, because the entropy integrand averages $\cos(\Delta\omega\,\tau)^2$ over the $\Phi$-grid — an integration that effectively lowpass-filters the oscillation.

We mitigated this in the PCRB runs with a small learning rate ($\mathrm{lr} = 10^{-4}$) and per-iter schedule reoptimization, producing a small but consistent ascent; Adam still cannot find the global $f_q^{\max} \to 30$~GHz optimum of the envelope because the step size is below the oscillation correlation length.  \emph{A proper PCRB-baseline solver would use a gradient-free global method (NLopt's DIRECT-L) or a smoothed objective.}  We note this honestly rather than engineering a more elaborate solver: the joint-DP machinery's envelope gradient is \emph{precisely} the right tool for this landscape, while the PCRB baseline inherits a standard multi-modality problem that is known to plague frequentist optimal-design formulations.

%====================================================================
\section{Switching joint-DP's terminal reward to $-\mathrm{Var}(b_K)$}
\label{sec:mse_terminal}
%====================================================================

\subsection{Motivation and derivation}

The Bayesian MSE of the posterior-mean estimator satisfies the tower-law identity
\begin{equation}
  \E_{\Phi, \bm y}\!\left[(\hat\Phi_{\text{pm}} - \Phi)^2\right]
  \;=\; \E_{\bm y}\!\left[\mathrm{Var}(\Phi \mid \bm y)\right],
\end{equation}
i.e., the deployment-time MSE equals the expected posterior variance at the terminal belief.  The plan's MI-based terminal reward $W_K(b_K) = -H(b_K)$ (plus additive constant) is \emph{asymptotically} MSE-equivalent (under Gaussian-posterior BvM), but at $K = 3$ on a bounded-support uniform prior the gap is first-order.

The fix is one line of code: replace the leaf reward with
\begin{equation}
  W_K^{\text{MSE}}(b_K) \;=\; -\mathrm{Var}(\Phi \mid b_K)
  \;=\; -\!\!\int (\Phi - \mu_{b_K})^2\, b_K(\Phi)\,d\Phi,
\end{equation}
so that $V_{\text{adaptive}}(\bm c) = \E[-\mathrm{Var}(b_K)]$ is $-1\times$ the deployed Bayesian MSE at the \emph{joint-DP-optimal} policy.  The envelope-theorem gradient $\partial V / \partial \bm c$ is unchanged in structure — only the leaf value changes.  We implemented this with a new \texttt{terminal::Symbol} keyword (\texttt{:mi} vs \texttt{:mse}) threading through \texttt{Bellman.jl}, \texttt{Gradient.jl}, and \texttt{JointOpt.jl}; the MI path is the default and unchanged.

The natural next interventions, in increasing order of implementation cost:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{$K \ge 4$ horizon:} the model-deployment gap closes with longer horizons (Bernstein--von Mises), and the adaptive margin over fixed schedules grows.  Memo size scales from $\sim 16\,$k at $K = 3$ to $\sim 200\,$k at $K = 4$; still tractable.
  \item \textbf{Richer action grid $J = 6, L = 4$:} $n_{\max}$ from $10$ to $30$ gives $3\!\times$ more shots per episode, collapsing posterior variance faster; the adaptive tree has more meaningful branching.
  \item \textbf{Tier-2 $\bm c$ ($+5$ SQUID-loop geometric dims):} gives joint-DP a high-dimensional geometric lever the single-level PCRB baseline cannot exploit.
  \item \textbf{Initialize from $\bm c^\star_2$:} doesn't help (see §13.4) without fixing the model-deployment gap.
\end{enumerate}
We report here rather than iterate further: the framework's §8.7 operational claim is not invalidated (it holds asymptotically and has been demonstrated in the literature for problems at larger $K$), but \emph{this} scqubit case study at $K = 3$ and tier-1 $\bm c$ does not exhibit it under the original configuration.

\section{Fine $\tau$ discretization: the claim holds}
\label{sec:fine}
%====================================================================

\subsection{Why the earlier runs failed to demonstrate the claim}

The $\tau$-grid in §\ref{sec:fq_sweep}--§\ref{sec:mse_terminal} was $J = 4$: $\tau \in \{20, 40, 80, 160\}$~ns.  Combined with $L = 2$ ($n \in \{1, 10\}$) this gives $|\mathcal{A}| = 8$ actions per epoch.  For the PCRB-optimal fixed schedule $(\tau_4, n_2)^K$ at baseline, the belief-conditional optimal action at \emph{every} reachable node is also $(\tau_4, n_2)$: the action set is too sparse to reward belief-dependent branching.  The adaptive policy collapses to the fixed schedule and inherits no advantage.

With a \emph{physically realistic} box (§13) and a \emph{fine} $\tau$ grid $J = 20$ log-spaced over $[10,\,320]$~ns (sub-percent $\tau$ resolution, well below the 6-MHz oscillation scale), the adaptive policy can finally choose \emph{different} $\tau$ at different belief states --- short $\tau$ early for coarse phase disambiguation, longer $\tau$ later for refinement.  This is what the count-statistic Bellman DP was designed to do.

\subsection{What changed, mechanistically}

Both designs converged to essentially the same geometry: $f_q^{\max} \approx 9.1$~GHz, $E_C/h \approx 0.256$~GHz (paper baseline values).  The 13.4\% gap is therefore entirely a \emph{policy-structure} effect:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item PCRB's optimal fixed schedule is $\bm s^\star_2 = (\tau_{20}, n_2)^3 = (320~\mathrm{ns}, 10)^3$: maximum $\tau$ and $n$ at every epoch, pinned by the asymptotic Fisher-info criterion.
  \item Joint-DP's adaptive policy varies $\tau_k$ across epochs based on belief: it allocates short $\tau$ when the posterior is still broad (to avoid phase-wrapping ambiguity) and transitions to long $\tau$ once a rough estimate is in hand.  This is exactly the Kitaev-ladder phase-estimation intuition, now \emph{belief-conditional} rather than prescribed.
  \item The 13.4\% gap is at $+11.54\sigma$ --- far beyond statistical noise --- and was invisible at $J = 4$ because the action set didn't admit this belief-conditional structure.
\end{itemize}

The framework's §8.7 claim holds \emph{at this problem} once three ingredients are in place:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Action set rich enough for belief-conditional choices} ($J \ge 10$--$20$, exceeding the $J = 4$ regime where adaptive collapses to fixed).
  \item \textbf{Terminal reward aligned with deployment metric} ($-\mathrm{Var}(b_K)$, not $-H(b_K)$, since the deployment metric is MSE of posterior mean, not mutual information).
  \item \textbf{Realistic $\bm c$ box} that doesn't let either optimizer escape through physically unattainable noise reduction.
\end{enumerate}
Absent any one of these, the claim is invisible.
